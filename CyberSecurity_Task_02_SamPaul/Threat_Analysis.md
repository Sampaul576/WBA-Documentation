# Threat Identification

## 1. Insider Threat

**Description:** A security risk that originates from within the organization — an employee, student, or contractor who misuses their authorized access, either intentionally (data theft, sabotage) or unintentionally (negligence, mistakes).

**Possible Impact:** Data leaks, sabotage of systems, financial loss, loss of trust — often harder to detect than external attacks since the person already has legitimate access.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Principle of least privilege (only give access needed for the role)
- Regular access audits and logging
- Employee background checks and security awareness training
- Monitoring for unusual access patterns

---

## 2. Malware

**Description:** Malicious software (viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, spyware) designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to systems.

**Possible Impact:** Data loss, system downtime, financial loss (ransom payments), data theft, spread to other connected systems.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Updated antivirus / endpoint protection software
- Regular system patching
- Avoiding untrusted downloads and email attachments
- Network segmentation to limit spread

---

## 3. Phishing

**Description:** A social engineering attack where attackers impersonate trusted entities (via email, SMS, or fake websites) to trick users into revealing credentials or sensitive information.

**Possible Impact:** Stolen credentials, unauthorized account access, financial fraud, further compromise of internal systems.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Security awareness training for staff and students
- Email filtering and anti-phishing tools
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) so stolen passwords alone aren't enough
- Verifying sender domains before clicking links

---

## 4. Weak Passwords

**Description:** The use of simple, reused, or easily guessable passwords that can be cracked through brute-force or dictionary attacks.

**Possible Impact:** Unauthorized account access, which can be a stepping stone to larger breaches across connected systems.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Enforced strong password policies (length, complexity)
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Password managers to avoid reuse
- Regular password expiry/rotation for critical systems

---

## 5. Unpatched Systems

**Description:** Software or operating systems that have not been updated with the latest security patches, leaving known vulnerabilities open for exploitation (as seen in the WannaCry case study).

**Possible Impact:** Exploitation of known vulnerabilities leading to malware infection, data breaches, or full system compromise.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Regular and timely patch management
- Automated update scheduling where possible
- Vulnerability scanning to identify outdated software
- Decommissioning unsupported legacy systems
